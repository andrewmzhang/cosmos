What is Metallicity?

Metallicity is a property of celestial objects that refers to the proportion of matter made of chemical elements heavier than Hydrogen & Helium. 

What is Metallicity used for?

Metallicity can be used to determine the age, composition, relative motion, density, mass, and temperature of a single star or a galaxy.

Our Project 

IRAF (image reduction and analysis facility) was a program used to measure Emission Lines such as H-alpha, H-beta, OII, OIII, and NII. Our group used this program to analyze spectra.

Spectra is obtained when...
The lens from a telescope collects light from a distant object & brings it to a focal point.
Diffraction occurs when light passes through thin slits in a metal plate set over the lens, separating the light into various wavelengths.

                 

                                                             (Our data was taken by the Keck telescope in Hawaii)

Before making calculations, we used the spectra to get information about

  • Observed Center Wavelength and Flux of Emission Lines of: OIII, OII, H-alpha, H-beta, NII

  • Wavelength

  • Rest Wavelength

  • Apparent Magnitude

for all 63 galaxies we were working with! 

 What did we calculate?

  • Redshift: When galaxies move away from us, the light waves are stretched out and shifted towards the "red" end of the spectrum. When this occurs, the observed wavelength is longer than the rest wavelength. 
           Redshift (z) = (observed wavelength - rest wavelength) / rest wavelength
  • Distance: Distance was calculated in terms of megaparsecs (3.08567758 × 1022 meters)
           First we calculate the velocity of the galaxy: redshift x constant of speed of light 
           And then we take the velocity and divide by the Hubble Constant H_0 km/s/Mpc, to determine the distance!
  • Absolute Magnitude: Galaxies that are further away tend to appear fainter, but the absolute magnitude informs us of how intrinisically bright the object is. 
            m_Abs = M_apparent - 5log(distance) + 5
  • Star Formation Rate:  Luminosity = (Halpha Flux) x 4 π d^2 and then SFR = Luminosity x (7.9 x 10^-42)
  • Metallicity: (12 + Log O/H) – (0.32 x O3N2)

Well that's dandy and all, but it probably doesn't make much sense unless we have some graphs to show the correlation!

 

 

Why does this even matter?

Through our metallicity calculations, we had access to various aspects of each galaxy, from redshift to lumonosity. So just by measuring the emission lines of the plasma surrounding all the stars in our given galaxies, we were able to collect a huge amount of information (63 galaxies!) and graph many relations. All of this data adds to the general knowledge of the astronomical community, and hopefully provides a basis on which even more, really amazing discoveries might be made.

Yay metallicity!

Further Research:

"It's not that I'm so smart , it's just that I stay with problems longer ." -Albert Einstein

If we could stay with this problem longer (I wish we had more time in COSMOS!), what would we do? We had two ideas:

One, we would want to create a program which, when given an object's metallicity, could calculate its different properties for us. Also, based on redshift and star formation rates, the program could also possibly predict what the object would be like billions of years from now. 

Our second idea is a little related to the exoplanets project. We would like to see if there was a relation between a population 1 star's metallicity and its ability to host rocky planets, gaseous planets, or both.
