Galactic Mass

What if I told you that we could determine the mass of a galaxy from light?

You probably don't believe me right now, but it's possible. In fact, we can determine more than just the mass of a galaxy. On this website, we're going to show you how we were able to do it, and how the process of the methods allowed us to learn so much more about galaxies.

Purpose: To learn more about galaxies, we use light in the form of spectra graphs to find the radius, rotation speed, and mass of distant galaxies. From that data, we can determine different things, such as the size of a galaxy, the # of stars in a galaxy, rotation speed (orbital velocity) of stars/gas in a galaxy and more.  

 

Spectroscopy:

Spectroscopy is the study of the interaction between matter and electromagnetic radiation. Spectra are taken with spectrographs and telescopes similar to the one pictured directly to the right. Spectra can tell us the composition, temperature, pressure, abundance of elements, and motion of an object. The image on the right, below the telescopes is an example of a picture of a galaxy in the form of a spectra. You may be wondering how can one get data from the spectra. The light that is being emitted from an object such as a galaxy is what is being recorded on the spectral data. Atoms have specific energy levels inside them where electrons move in orbitals around the nucleus. When an electron moves down to a lower energy level, energy in the form of light 

is emitted from the atom. The energy levels in an atom are quantized which means that there are specific energy levels that an electron can be at. Since energy levels are quantized, only specific wavelengths can be emitted from an atom. Since we know specific wavelengths for certain elements, we can use this knowledge to find what galaxies are made of.

 

Emission Patterns: 

We looked at three specific patterns when searching for our data:

1. The first pattern we looked at was the Oxygen 2 (OII) pattern. As pictured below, it looks like two dots right next to each other. Each of the dots, or emission lines, have a wavelength of about 3727 Ångstroms.

 

2. The second pattern we looked at was the Beta Particle (Hβ) and Oxygen 3 (OIII) triplet. This pattern has three particles in a row with the Beta Particle being the leftmost dot and the two Oxygen III particles on the right. The distance between the Beta Particle is two times the distance between the two Oxygen III emission lines. The emission lines at rest have a wavelength of 4861 Ångstroms, 4959 Ångstroms and 5007 Ångstroms respectively. An example of this pattern is pictured below.

  

3. The last pattern we observed was the Alpha Particle (Hα), Nitrogen II (NII) and Sulfur II (SII) emission pattern. This pattern has five particles in a row in a pattern like the one pictured below. The three dots on the left are two nitrogen emission lines with an alpha particle in the middle and the two on the right are sulfur emission lines. The emission lines at rest have wavelengths of 6584 Ångstroms, 6563 Ångstroms, 6584 Ångstroms, 6716 Ångstroms, and 6731 Ångstroms respectively.

 

Differential Doppler Shift:

The differential Doppler shift is the change in frequency of waves when the wave’s observermoves towards or away from it. The image below exhibits this phenomenon. For example, as the source of a car horn approaches you, it is heard at a higher pitch than when it moves away from you. The Doppler shift applies to allelectromagnetic waves, including light.This phenomenon can be observed visually through the concepts ofredshift and blueshift.

 

 

Redshift:

 

As an object in space moves away from the observer, it appears red in color.This is due to the extensionin wavelength from the light source to theobserver.The opposite (blueshift) happens as the source and observer are closer to each other, resulting in a shorter wavelength between the two. In our research we found the redshift by finding the wavelength of the actual emission line and compared it to the wavelength of the emission line at rest (see emission patterns).

Data and Conclusion:

The masses of the galaxies we measured are all fairly similar in that they have masses that are much less than those of the Andromeda (~1E+12 M☉) and Milky Way (1.0–1.5E+12 M☉) galaxies. The calculated mass values are expectedly lower because the galaxies we observed are more faint (not viewable with human eye) and further away. Because of the distance and the time it takes for light to travel long distances, the galaxies appear young and have not experienced much of what happens in life, that is, colliding with other galaxies (a phenomenon which repeatedly happens with time). Our data is biased towards lower values for the following reasons. 

  • Reason 1: We may be observing the inner stars which do not have high orbital velocity, thus the redshift is not apparent.

  • Reason 2: The way it’s spinning to our line of sight. If we look at the galaxy and see the spiral face-on, then there won’t be redshift and as a result, no rotation will be visible.
