What Are Eclipsing Binaries?

Eclisping binaries are a type of a binary star system whose orbits are at an angle so that, from Earth, one passes in front of the other, causing an eclipse. And a binary star is star system consisting of two stars orbiting around their common center of mass. The more massive star is the primary star and the other is its companion star or secondary.

Types of eclipsing binaries:

  • Detached - The class of which most binaries belong to. Both stars are relatively unaffected by the other. 
  • Semi-Detached - One of the binary star components is being sucked in, but the other's is not.
  • Contact - A binary system in which the components of the stars are being sucked in by the gravity.
  • Near Contact - 
  • Over Contact - Binary system whose stars share an envelope.

There are also some other types of binary stars like visual binaries, spectorscopic binaries and astrometric binaries.

 

Spectroscopic --> Too far away to detect as a binary. As a result, it uses the dopplar shift effect to detect the orbit of the stars.

Red shift shows it is moving away, and blue shift shows it's moving closer.

Astrometric --> Companion stars of a binary are found through the observation of slight perturbations or "wobbles" due to the effect

of gravity from each star.

 

Visual --> Binary system which, as the name suggests, the individual stars can be observed. Careful observations over time will show whether 

the stars are orbiting each other or not.

Light Curves of Eclipsing Binaries

Since eclipsing binaries stars are very distant from Earth, they often appear as only one light source, but we still know its a binary system with the help of light curves.

An example of a light curve:

First of all, there are two dips in this graph, each representing eclipse (light level drops because one star is covered by another), and one dip is drops more drasticly than the other because the brighter star is covered, when there is no eclipse the light level generally stays the same. Although stars are still moving away or towards us (stars' circular motion), those light level changes become very insignificant due to huge distances between us and the stars.

Tabletop Experiment

Observing a real eclipsing binary star system generally takes a long time, but an valid way to simulate a light curve is by doing a tabletop experiment.

Two light bulbs represent two stars and the light meter on the left acts like a telescope. This particular setup simulates an eclipsing binary system and generates a light curve (note that the two light bulbs are both 25W and the graph has two periods).

This simulated curve looks pretty close to the actual curve - it has two dips for each period. However, the part where no eclipse occurs is not so flat. That is because the distance each light bulb moves matter now since the light meter is very close to the source of light.

Here is another graph simulated using this system, using one 95W light bulb and a 25W light bulbs, instead of two identical ones (note this graph only has one period).

The two dips are still there even though one is dramatically shallower than the other, but this time on the right part of the graph the light level goes up and back down. This happens when the 95W light bulb moves closer to the light meter and dominates the sensors.
